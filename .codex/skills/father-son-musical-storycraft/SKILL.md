# Father-Son Musical Storycraft

Use this skill to develop and revise the father/son musical story arc in `docs/story-arc.md`.

## Workflow

1. Read and follow [references/role-prompt.md](references/role-prompt.md) as the active role/system prompt before making story decisions.
2. Read `docs/story-arc.md` before modifying existing material. If it does not exist, create it using [references/story-arc-format.md](references/story-arc-format.md).
3. Use [references/storycraft-workflow.md](references/storycraft-workflow.md) for musical-theatre structure, song function, character want, conflict, reversals, reprises, and emotional payoff.
4. Use [references/two-voice-guitar-motif.md](references/two-voice-guitar-motif.md) for the father/son two-voice or two-guitar dramatic language.
5. Apply the requested change while preserving unaffected continuity.
6. Update `docs/story-arc.md` directly. Keep it human-readable and structured.
7. Record every meaningful change in the `Revision Notes` section with the date and a concise description.

## Required Story Commitments

- The musical follows a father and son from before the child is born through the son's departure for college and independent adulthood.
- Voice/Guitar 1 represents the father. It begins mature, harmonically grounded, and capable of lead expression.
- Voice/Guitar 2 represents the son. It enters after the child's arrival, starts simply, grows through imitation and resistance, and eventually can surpass or transform Voice/Guitar 1.
- Story decisions must connect emotional growth to musical growth.
- Do not discard established continuity unless the user explicitly asks for a reset.

## Editing Rules

- When revising a beat, update affected act, scene, song, motif, and character sections together.
- If a user request conflicts with the lifecycle arc or two-voice/guitar progression, explain the conflict and offer a compatible alternative before overwriting the core premise.
- Keep lyric drafts, full scenes, and notation out of `docs/story-arc.md` unless the user explicitly asks for those deliverables.
- Prefer concrete theatrical choices over abstract advice: identify who wants what, what blocks them, why the moment must become music, and what changes by the end.
